Bio

I am a researcher specializing in water resources management, with an academic foundation rooted in environmental sustainability and interdisciplinary engineering. My work focuses on the intersection of water, industry, and environmental protection, with particular emphasis on reducing freshwater demand through innovative reuse strategies. My academic journey began in biomedical engineering, where I was first exposed to the environmental footprint of healthcare systems.

My Research Journey

During my undergraduate studies, I became increasingly aware of the adverse impacts of hospital and laboratory waste on ecosystems and public health. Hazardous chemical effluents, pharmaceutical residues entering water bodies, single-use plastics, heavy metals, disinfectants, and improperly treated infectious waste all contribute to soil degradation, aquatic toxicity, antimicrobial resistance, and long-term contamination of natural resources. These observations were no longer abstract environmental concerns, but they represented a structural challenge embedded within essential human services.

After graduation, I worked in a dental equipment company in Iran. This professional experience deepened my awareness of the environmental consequences of medical and dental practices. Dental amalgam waste containing mercury, chemical sterilization by-products, plastic-based disposable instruments, wastewater contaminated with metals and pathogens, and energy-intensive manufacturing processes highlighted the unavoidable yet significant ecological burden of the healthcare supply chain. It became clear to me that I could not remain indifferent to these impacts. This realization marked a decisive turning point in my life: I chose to redirect my career toward environmental sustainability. Seeking a pathway into this field, I pursued a Master’s degree in Construction Project Management. I intentionally selected this interdisciplinary program because it offered comprehensive exposure to sustainability principles, risk assessment, environmental management, health and safety regulations, and the lifecycle impacts of construction activities.

Throughout my graduate studies, my academic interests progressively converged on one critical and increasingly threatened natural resource: water. Recognizing water scarcity as one of the defining challenges of our time, I focused my MSc dissertation on the environmental impacts of concrete production, particularly examining water consumption patterns and the critical role of water quality in sustainable concrete manufacturing in the UK. My research, titled “Analytical and Statistical Study of Producing Concrete Using Different Types of Wastewater Resources,” investigated the feasibility of substituting freshwater with treated wastewater in concrete production.

The study evaluated wastewater classification, treatment standards, physicochemical properties, and their implications for material performance and environmental sustainability. By addressing global water scarcity and promoting wastewater reuse in industrial processes, the research contributed to strategies aimed at mitigating freshwater depletion and reducing environmental pressures within the construction sector. Although my thesis was positioned within the construction domain, it reaffirmed that my primary intellectual commitment lies directly within water resources management. Water is not only a material input in industrial systems; it is the foundation of ecological balance, public health, and human survival.

Future Research Directions

My long-term academic vision is to pursue doctoral research in water resources management and contribute to evidence-based solutions that protect and sustain this vital resource. I aspire to become a university professor within the next four to five years, dedicating my career to research, teaching, and advancing sustainable water management practices that safeguard both present and future generations. For me, water is more than a research field; it is a responsibility.

  • MSc Thesis: This MSc study evaluates the feasibility of replacing potable mixing water with treated wastewater in concrete production. The research focuses on the influence of wastewater quality parameters on key performance indicators, particularly compressive strength and durability. Through analytical assessment of existing standards and previously reported findings, the study examines the technical and environmental implications of wastewater reuse in concrete mixtures. The results suggest that, when quality-controlled and properly treated, certain categories of wastewater can be utilized without significant adverse effects on mechanical performance or long-term durability. The study supports wastewater reuse as a viable strategy within sustainable concrete technology and integrated water resource management frameworks.
